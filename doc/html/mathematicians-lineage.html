🌳 The Four RH Routes: Parallel Genealogies

Instead of one proof path, mathematicians have identified four conceptually independent routes to RH. Each has its own lineage, key figures, and current state.

🟢 Functional Route (Nyman–Beurling)

Key Figures: Nyman (1950), Beurling (1955), Báez-Duarte (2003), Vasyunin (1999–2015)

Current Status: Most formalization-friendly. H13+H14 proven in Lean 4; H15 (quadratic cancellation) is open research.

Why This Route: Reformulates RH as a Hilbert-space closure property. Direct connection to analytic number theory via cotangent sums.

🟣 Spectral Route (Hilbert–Pólya)

Key Figures: Hilbert (1900), Pólya (1927), de Branges (1990s), Connes (2000s)

Current Status: Open. Seeks a self-adjoint operator whose eigenvalues are the RH zeros.

Why This Route: If true, explains zero-spacing statistics via spectral theory. Deep connection to quantum mechanics.

🟠 Analytic Route (Robin–Lagarias)

Key Figures: Riemann (1859), Robin (1885), Lagarias (2002), Chowla–Briggs (modern)

Current Status: Open. RH equivalent to arithmetic inequalities on divisor sums.

Why This Route: Elementary formulation. RH ⟺ σ(n) < e^γ · n · log log n for all n > 5040.

🔴 Probabilistic Route (Montgomery–GUE)

Key Figures: Riemann (1859), Montgomery (1973), Katz–Sarnak (1990s), Keating–Snaith (2000s)

Current Status: Not provably equivalent but best empirical fit. Zeros behave like random matrix eigenvalues.

Why This Route: Deepest numerical support. GUE (Gaussian Unitary Ensemble) predictions match zero spacing perfectly.

1800s 19th Century: Birth of the Hypothesis
Bernhard Riemann
1826–1866
Founding Question (1859)
Posed the Riemann Hypothesis in 1859 as a remark in his paper on prime distribution. Introduced the zeta function as a complex variable and conjectured all non-trivial zeros lie on Re(s) = 1/2. The hypothesis emerged from his explicit formula connecting primes to zeros.
Explicit Formulas Prime Distribution Zeta Zeros
1859: On the Number of Primes Less Than a Given Quantity
Jacques Hadamard
1865–1963
Prime Number Theorem (1896)
Proved the Prime Number Theorem (1896) using RH-adjacent techniques. Showed that all non-trivial zeros of ζ(s) must lie on or near the critical line. Established foundational zero-free region methods that would shape quantitative RH research for a century.
Zero-Free Regions PNT Proof Analytic Method
1896: Prime Number Theorem proof
Charles de la Vallée Poussin
1866–1962
Zero-Free Region (1896)
Independently proved PNT (1896) and developed the De la Vallée Poussin zero-free region—a quantitative boundary that remains standard today. This region is central to unconditional bounds on Möbius functions and modern RH equivalences. His work enabled H14's DVP-based approach.
DVP Region Quantitative Bounds Classical Foundation
1896: PNT proof & DVP region (used in H14)
1900–1950 Early–Mid 20th Century: Spectral & Functional Foundations
George Pólya
1887–1985
Spectral Interpretation (1927)
Suggested that RH might follow from the existence of a self-adjoint operator whose spectrum consists precisely of the RH zeros. This speculation, made early in the century, inspired the Hilbert–Pólya program and all modern spectral approaches to RH. One of four independent routes.
Spectral Route Self-Adjoint Operator Quantum Bridge
1927: Hilbert–Pólya conjecture (speculative)
Alf Beurling
1912–1982
Functional Criterion (1955)
Developed the Nyman–Beurling criterion rigorously in 1955, proving RH is equivalent to density of certain sequences in L²(0,∞). Showed how to formalize RH without invoking zeta zeros explicitly, creating an alternative proof pathway purely through functional analysis. Foundation of Phase NB.
Functional Route Hilbert Space Closure Properties
1955: Nyman–Beurling criterion (formalization-friendly)
Harald Nyman
1929–2000
Hilbert Space Reformulation (1950)
Reformulated RH using Hilbert-space closure properties of fractional parts (1950). Showed RH is equivalent to the density of shifted unit fractions in L²(0,∞). This functional-analytic approach opened new proof strategies independent of analytic continuation—enabling computational verification.
Functional Analysis Closure in L² Fractional Parts
1950: RH ⟺ closure in L²—basis of Phase NB
1950–2000 Late 20th Century: Explicit Bounds & Statistical Routes
Hugh Montgomery
1944–present
GUE Hypothesis (1973)
Discovered that zero-spacing statistics match the GUE (Gaussian Unitary Ensemble) from random matrix theory (1973). Showed empirical evidence that RH zeros behave like eigenvalues of random Hermitian matrices—a profound connection between number theory and quantum physics. One of four independent routes.
Probabilistic Route Random Matrices GUE Distribution
1973: Montgomery pair correlation hypothesis
Bernard Vasyunin
1951–present
Explicit Cotangent Sums (1999–2015)
Made the Nyman–Beurling criterion computational by explicitly formulating it as cotangent sums (1999–2015). Derived exact identities connecting period functions, Möbius coefficients, and zeta zeros. Central figure in bridging classical and functional routes. Foundation of H13 in our project.
Vasyunin Identity Cotangent Sums Computational Form
1999–2015: Cotangent sums framework (H13 basis)
Luis Báez-Duarte
1939–present
BBLS Formulation (2003)
Simplified Vasyunin's approach into the modern BBLS (Balazard–Saias–Landreau–Saias) formulation (2003). Proved the strong criterion: RH holds iff the norm of the weighted Möbius sum is O(1/log N). This enabled the first Lean 4 formalizations and is the foundation of H13 completion.
BBLS Chain O(1/log N) Bound Formalizable
2003: BBLS criterion (H13 formalization basis)
2000–2026 Contemporary: Bilinear Cancellation & Formalization
Stefano Bettin & Brian Conrey
1980s–present
Period Functions & Cancellation (2013)
Analyzed the bilinear structure of Möbius–Vasyunin interactions (2013). Showed cancellation in period-function sums is essential to achieving O(1/log N) bounds. Their work opened H15 (quadratic cancellation) as an independent open problem, revealing why linear bounds alone are insufficient for final proof.
Möbius Cancellation Period Functions H15 Problem
2013: Approximations to RH (H15 research basis)
Alain Connes
1947–present
Spectral Geometry (1998–2007)
Developed noncommutative geometry perspectives on RH (1998–2007). Connected zeta zeros to the structure of adele rings and spectral triples. Proposed geometric interpretations of why RH might be true from deep symmetry principles—bridging analytic number theory and quantum physics. Modern spectral route.

1998–2007: Noncommutative geometry & RH (spectral route)

The Genealogical Network

What emerges is not a single ancestor but a genealogical network where four conceptually independent routes developed in parallel:

  • Classical Route (Riemann → Hadamard → DVP): Prime number distribution and zero-free regions—the analytic foundation.
  • Functional Route (Nyman → Beurling → Báez-Duarte → Vasyunin): Hilbert-space closure properties—the formalization-friendly path. This is where our Phase NB resides.
  • Spectral Route (Pólya → Connes): Self-adjoint operators and noncommutative geometry—the quantum bridge.
  • Probabilistic Route (Montgomery → Katz–Sarnak): Random matrix theory and zero-spacing statistics—the empirical record.

Our project formalizes the Functional Route (most Lean-friendly) while acknowledging that the Spectral and Probabilistic routes may eventually provide orthogonal insights. The convergence of four routes toward the same conjecture suggests something deep is true—we just haven't found the right language to prove it yet.
