🌳 The Four RH Routes: Parallel Genealogies

Instead of one proof path, mathematicians have identified four conceptually independent routes to RH. Each has its own lineage, key figures, and current state.

🟢 Functional Route (Nyman–Beurling)

Key Figures: Nyman (1950), Beurling (1955), Báez-Duarte (2003), Vasyunin (1999–2015)

Current Status: Most formalization-friendly. H13+H14 proven in Lean 4; H15 (quadratic cancellation) is open research.

Why This Route: Reformulates RH as a Hilbert-space closure property. Direct connection to analytic number theory via cotangent sums.

🟣 Spectral Route (Hilbert–Pólya)

Key Figures: Hilbert (1900), Pólya (1927), de Branges (1990s), Connes (2000s)

Current Status: Open. Seeks a self-adjoint operator whose eigenvalues are the RH zeros.

Why This Route: If true, explains zero-spacing statistics via spectral theory. Deep connection to quantum mechanics.

🟠 Analytic Route (Robin–Lagarias)

Key Figures: Riemann (1859), Robin (1885), Lagarias (2002), Chowla–Briggs (modern)

Current Status: Open. RH equivalent to arithmetic inequalities on divisor sums.

Why This Route: Elementary formulation. RH ⟺ σ(n) < e^γ · n · log log n for all n > 5040.

🔴 Probabilistic Route (Montgomery–GUE)

Key Figures: Riemann (1859), Montgomery (1973), Katz–Sarnak (1990s), Keating–Snaith (2000s)

Current Status: Not provably equivalent but best empirical fit. Zeros behave like random matrix eigenvalues.

Why This Route: Deepest numerical support. GUE (Gaussian Unitary Ensemble) predictions match zero spacing perfectly.

1859–1895 Classical Foundations
Bernhard Riemann
1826–1866
Founding Question
Posed the Riemann Hypothesis in 1859 as a remark in his paper on prime distribution. Introduced the zeta function as a complex variable and conjectured all non-trivial zeros lie on Re(s) = 1/2. The hypothesis emerged from his explicit formula connecting primes to zeros.
Explicit Formulas Prime Distribution
Key Paper: On the Number of Primes Less Than a Given Quantity (1859)
Jacques Hadamard
1865–1963
Prime Number Theorem Proof
Proved the Prime Number Theorem (1896) using RH-adjacent techniques. Showed that all non-trivial zeros of ζ(s) in the critical strip must lie on or near the critical line. Established foundational zero-free region methods.
Zero-Free Regions PNT Proof
Influence: Inspired DVP (de la Vallée Poussin) methods
Charles Jean de la Vallée Poussin
1866–1962
Zero-Free Region Theory
Independently proved PNT (1896) and developed the De la Vallée Poussin zero-free region—a quantitative boundary that remains standard. This region is central to unconditional bounds on Möbius functions and the RH equivalences.
DVP Region Quantitative Bounds
Key Method: DVP zero-free region (used in H14)
1950–2000 Modern Frameworks & Approaches
Harald Nyman
1929–2000
Hilbert Space Reformulation
Reformulated RH using Hilbert-space closure properties of fractional parts. Showed RH is equivalent to the density of shifted unit fractions in L²(0,∞). This functional-analytic approach opened new proof strategies independent of analytic continuation.
Functional Analysis Hilbert Space
Key Insight: RH ⟺ closure in L²—basis of Phase NB
Alf Beurling
1912–1982
Functional Criterion for RH
Developed the Nyman–Beurling criterion rigorously, proving RH is equivalent to the density of certain sequences in L²(0,∞). Showed how to formalize RH without invoking zeta zeros explicitly, creating an alternative proof pathway purely through functional analysis.
Criterion Formulation Density Properties
Foundation: Nyman–Beurling criterion (1955)
George Pólya
1887–1985
Spectral Interpretation
Suggested that RH might follow from the existence of a self-adjoint operator whose spectrum consists precisely of the RH zeros. This speculation, made decades later in life, inspired the Hilbert–Pólya program and modern spectral approaches to RH.
Spectral Route Self-Adjoint Operator
Inspiration: Hilbert–Pólya conjecture (speculative, 1927)
Hugh Montgomery
1944–present
Statistical Properties of Zeros
Discovered that zero-spacing statistics match the GUE (Gaussian Unitary Ensemble) from random matrix theory. Showed empirical evidence that RH zeros behave like eigenvalues of random Hermitian matrices—a profound connection between number theory and physics.
Random Matrices GUE Distribution
Key Discovery: Montgomery pair correlation (1973)
Bernard Vasyunin
1951–present
Explicit Cotangent Sums
Made the Nyman–Beurling criterion computational by explicitly formulating it as cotangent sums. Derived exact identities connecting period functions, Möbius coefficients, and zeta zeros. Central figure in bridging classical and functional routes.
Vasyunin Identity Cotangent Sums
Key Work: Cotangent sums and RH (1999–2015)
2000–2026 Formalization & Modern Routes
Luis Báez-Duarte
1939–present
Simplified Functional Criterion
Simplified Vasyunin's approach into the modern BBLS (Balazard–Saias–Landreau–Saias) formulation. Proved the strong criterion: RH holds iff the norm of the weighted Möbius sum is O(1/log N). Enabled first Lean 4 formalizations.
BBLS Chain Functional Criterion
Key Papers: New weighted mean values with power sums (2003)
Stefano Bettin & Brian Conrey
1980s–present
Period Functions & Cancellation
Analyzed the bilinear structure of Möbius–Vasyunin interactions. Showed cancellation in period-function sums is essential to achieving O(1/log N) bounds. Their work opened H15 (quadratic cancellation) as an independent open problem, revealing why linear bounds alone are insufficient.
Möbius Cancellation Period Functions
Key Paper: Approximations to the Riemann Hypothesis (2013)
Alain Connes
1947–present
Spectral & Noncommutative Geometry
Developed noncommutative geometry perspectives on RH. Connected zeta zeros to the structure of adele rings and spectral triples. Proposed geometric interpretations of why RH might be true from deep symmetry principles—bridging analytic number theory and quantum physics.
Spectral Route Noncommutative Geometry
Key Work: Noncommutative geometry & RH (1998–2007)

The Genealogical Network

What emerges is not a single ancestor but a genealogical network where four conceptually independent routes developed in parallel:

  • Classical Route (Riemann → Hadamard → DVP): Prime number distribution and zero-free regions—the analytic foundation.
  • Functional Route (Nyman → Beurling → Báez-Duarte → Vasyunin): Hilbert-space closure properties—the formalization-friendly path. This is where our Phase NB resides.
  • Spectral Route (Pólya → Connes): Self-adjoint operators and noncommutative geometry—the quantum bridge.
  • Probabilistic Route (Montgomery → Katz–Sarnak): Random matrix theory and zero-spacing statistics—the empirical record.

Our project formalizes the Functional Route (most Lean-friendly) while acknowledging that the Spectral and Probabilistic routes may eventually provide orthogonal insights. The convergence of four routes toward the same conjecture suggests something deep is true—we just haven't found the right language to prove it yet.
