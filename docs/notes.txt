**Verdict:** this is a strong *verification-and-triage roadmap*, but it overstates its force as an actual RH attack. The best framing is not “Aristotle-Lean proves RH,” but:

> **Use Aristotle + Lean to formalize known RH-equivalent frameworks, expose missing hypotheses, and reject flawed proof attempts with machine-level rigor.**

The Aristotle description is broadly plausible: the official report says Aristotle uses Lean proof search, Monte Carlo Graph Search, lemma-based informal reasoning, formal feedback, and test-time training; it only counts a problem as solved when it produces complete Lean 4 code without `sorry` or unsound axioms. ([arXiv][1]) But current systems are still weak on research-level mathematics: the ArXivLean benchmark reports all models below 20% on recent research-level Lean problems, and says present LLMs cannot yet formally prove most research-level math with reasonable budgets. ([MathArena][2])

### 1. “Slow-Growing Bound” Verifier — **good, but not an RH engine**

This is a legitimate and useful direction. Numerical verification of many zeros does not address arbitrarily large heights; for example, Platt–Trudgian-style computations verified all zeros up to height about (3\times 10^{12}), corresponding to more than (12) trillion zeros, but this is still finite evidence. ([arXiv][3])

The weakness: zero-free regions and explicit analytic estimates are not “almost RH.” They usually prove statements near (\Re(s)=1), not the critical line (\Re(s)=1/2). Lean can force every asymptotic constant and limiting step to be honest, but it cannot make a weak theorem strong enough.

**Evaluation:** excellent as a formal explicit-bounds project; weak as a direct RH strategy.

### 2. Spectral Translation Engine — **conceptually exciting, mathematically fragile**

This is valuable because operator-theoretic RH attempts often fail at exactly the points Lean is good at checking: domain definitions, essential self-adjointness, spectrum, trace class assumptions, and whether the operator really has the claimed eigenvalues.

But the proposal currently hides the central missing theorem. Hilbert–Pólya needs a genuine self-adjoint operator whose spectrum gives the imaginary parts of the nontrivial zeros. Berry–Keating’s (H=xp) is historically important, but the literature still treats it as speculative; even reviews note that modified (xp) models reproduce average behavior but do not recover the exact zeros. ([Springer Nature Link][4])

**Evaluation:** excellent for falsifying spectral proposals; not yet a constructive route unless someone supplies the operator and the exact spectral theorem.

### 3. Convergence and Positivity Checker — **the strongest of the five**

This is the most credible Lean use case. RH-equivalent criteria often reduce to positivity, trace formulas, kernel positivity, or infinite sums/products. These are exactly where informal proofs often make illicit moves: exchanging limits, assuming absolute convergence, treating a non-trace-class operator as trace-class, or proving positivity only on a dense subspace without closure control.

The Polya–Szego claim should be phrased carefully: a January 2026 report says Aristotle verified 80 submitted Polya–Szego formalizations after an initial 2.8% Lean-proof rate, but that is a much narrower benchmark than RH-level analytic number theory. ([Igor Rivin][5])

**Evaluation:** best practical proposal. It will not prove RH by itself, but it could decisively expose the first false step in many claimed proofs.

### 4. Heuristic-to-Rigor Compiler — **useful, but the wording is too strong**

The diagnosis is right: probabilistic models of Möbius randomness, pair correlation, and random matrix theory are powerful heuristics but are not direct proofs. Lean could help formalize exactly what a conjectural probabilistic statement implies.

But “translate statistical models into deterministic bounds” is the hard theorem, not an engineering step. Lean can encode the gap between “almost surely in a model” and “for all arithmetic objects,” but it will not automatically bridge that gap.

**Evaluation:** good as a dependency mapper. Rephrase it as: “formalize which deterministic lemmas would be sufficient to turn the heuristic into a proof.”

### 5. Cross-Disciplinary Unifier — **good ambition, but overclaims about foundations**

This is attractive, especially for Connes-style approaches. Recent work by Connes, Consani, and Moscovici constructs self-adjoint operators from finite Euler products whose spectra approximate low zeta zeros, but explicitly says that a rigorous convergence proof would be needed to establish RH. ([arXiv][6]) Connes’ 2026 survey also frames the newest direction around Weil’s quadratic form, finite approximations, trace formulas, information theory, and convergence from finite to infinite Euler products. ([arXiv][7])

The problem: Lean does not magically unify fields unless the needed libraries exist. Noncommutative geometry, trace formulas, spectral triples, adèles, distributions, and analytic number theory would require enormous formal infrastructure.

**Evaluation:** excellent long-term formalization vision; too optimistic as an automated near-term proof path.

## Main correction I would make

Replace the grand claim:

> “Aristotle-Lean can attack RH by automatically translating all deep approaches.”

with the sharper and more credible claim:

> “Aristotle-Lean can turn RH approaches into explicit formal dependency graphs, verify all standard analytic/operator-theoretic steps already known, and isolate the exact missing theorem—typically a convergence, positivity, trace-class, or spectral-realization statement.”

## Ranking

1. **Convergence and Positivity Checker** — most realistic and valuable.
2. **Slow-Growing Bound Verifier** — excellent for explicit analytic estimates.
3. **Cross-Disciplinary Unifier** — powerful long-term project, huge infrastructure cost.
4. **Spectral Translation Engine** — useful for checking proposed operators, but missing the operator.
5. **Heuristic-to-Rigor Compiler** — conceptually helpful, but the hardest part is still mathematical invention.

So: **very good as a Lean-based RH “proof-auditing machine”; not yet credible as a Lean-based RH “proof-discovery machine.”**
